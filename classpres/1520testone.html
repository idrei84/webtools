Vocabulary in Context

What is it?

Using clues from the text to understand the meaning of unfamiliar words without looking up definitions.

üîë Key Strategies:

  • Look for definition clues (words that explain the meaning)
  • Find synonym clues (words with similar meanings)
  • Use contrast clues (opposite meanings)
  • Consider the overall context of the sentence/paragraph

Example:

"The teacher was perplexed by the student's answer‚Äîshe couldn't understand how he reached that conclusion."

Context clue: "couldn't understand" helps us know that perplexed means confused.

    Main Ideas

    What is a Main Idea?

    The most important point or central message that the author wants to communicate in a paragraph or passage.

    üéØ How to Find Main Ideas:

    • Look for topic sentences (often the first sentence)
    • Ask: "What is this paragraph mostly about?"
    • Find the idea that connects all the details
    • Check if your main idea is too broad or too narrow

    Example Paragraph:

    "Regular exercise provides many health benefits. It strengthens the heart and improves circulation. Exercise also helps maintain a healthy weight and reduces stress. Additionally, physical activity can improve sleep quality and boost energy levels."

    Main Idea: Regular exercise provides many health benefits.

    Supporting Details

    What are Supporting Details?

    Specific information that explains, proves, or gives examples of the main idea.

    üîç Types of Supporting Details:

    • Examples: Specific instances that illustrate the main idea
    • Statistics: Numbers and data that support the point
    • Facts: Information that can be proven true
    • Explanations: Details that clarify or expand the main idea

    Example:

    Main Idea: "Studying abroad offers valuable experiences."

    Supporting Details:

    • Students learn about different cultures
    • They become more independent
    • Language skills improve through daily practice
    • International experience helps in future careers

    • Implied Main Ideas

    What is an Implied Main Idea?

    A main idea that is suggested or hinted at but not directly stated in the text. You must "read between the lines" to understand it.

    üí≠ How to Find Implied Main Ideas:

    • Look at all the supporting details
    • Ask: "What do all these details have in common?"
    • Think about what the author is trying to suggest
    • Create a sentence that connects all the details

    Example:

    "Maria arrived at work 30 minutes late. Her hair was messy, and her clothes were wrinkled. She spilled coffee on her desk and forgot about an important meeting. When her boss asked her a question, she couldn't remember the answer."

    Implied Main Idea: Maria was having a bad day / Maria was not prepared for work.

    Test-Taking Strategies

    üìù Before You Read:

    • Preview the questions to know what to look for
    • Scan for key words from the questions
    • Predict what the passage might be about

    üìñ While You Read:

    • Read actively‚Äîunderline key information
    • Pay attention to first and last sentences of paragraphs
    • Look for transition words (however, therefore, for example)
    • Don't get stuck on difficult words

    ‚úÖ When Answering:

    • Eliminate obviously wrong answer choices
    • Go back to the text to check your answers
    • For vocabulary questions, substitute each answer choice
    • Make sure main ideas aren't too specific or too general
