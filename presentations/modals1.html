Modals Review

Chapter 9: Necessity, Advisability, Ability, Possibility, Requests & Suggestions

Basic Modal Rules

Modal Auxiliaries

can, could, may, might, must, shall, should, will, would, ought to, had better

Never change form regardless of subject

Phrasal Modals

be able to, be going to, be supposed to, have to, have got to

Change form based on subject (because they contain be/have)

Verb Form

Modal auxiliaries + base form of verb

Exception: ought to + infinitive

Phrasal Modals

Also followed by base form of verb

They conjugate, but the main verb doesn't change

Necessity

must / have to

Same meaning in positive form

must = more formal, stronger

have to = more common

have got to

Used in spoken English

Usually contracted: I've gotta go

Pronunciation: /hæftə/ or /gɑtə/

Examples:

  • She has to put gas in her car, or she won't make it home.
  • Students must demonstrate proficiency to proceed.
  • I've got to call my aunt to see how she's doing.

Lack of Necessity vs Prohibition

Lack of Necessity

don't have to

= not necessary, but allowed/OK

You can do it if you want to

Prohibition

must not

= cannot be done

Negative consequences if you do it

Examples:

  • Lack of necessity: You don't have to submit today, but you can if you want.
  • Prohibition: You must not submit others' work as your own.

Advisability

should / ought to

Same meaning - giving advice

ought to = more casual

Pronunciation: /ɔtə/ "oughta"

had better

Strong advice

Implies negative consequences if not followed

Usually contracted: you'd better

could

Used for suggestions

Especially to suggest options

Less strong than should/ought to

Examples:

  • You should submit assignments on time.
  • He ought to look for a new job if he's unhappy.
  • You'd better slow down, or you'll get pulled over.
  • We could remodel the whole kitchen.

Expectation

be supposed to

Formal expectations

What someone expects to happen

Based on rules, plans, or arrangements

should

General expectations

What normally happens

Based on knowledge or experience

Examples:

  • I'm supposed to go out of town this weekend.
  • He's supposed to be in the office by 8:30.
  • My son should be home from school soon.
  • The package should arrive today.

Ability

can

General ability

Physical or mental capability

Most common for ability

be able to

Same as "can" in most cases

More formal

Negative: not able to OR unable to

know how to

Learned ability

Based on knowledge/skill

Not exactly a modal, but similar meaning

Examples:

  • The gymnast can do a double backflip.
  • The three of us are able to lift this heavy box.
  • He knows how to play guitar, bass, and drums.
  • I'm not able to help you today.

Possibility

can

General possibility

What is sometimes true

Not necessarily always true

may

50% possibility

Something might happen

More formal than "might"

might

Less than 50% possibility

Something could happen

Less certain than "may"

Examples:

  • A new car can be more expensive than people think.
  • He may be on the road already.
  • She might come to the party, but I'm not sure.
  • This might make you feel better, or it might not.

Requests and Permission

Permission (I as subject)

May I = most polite

Could I = polite

Can I = most informal

Add "please" for extra politeness

Requests (You as subject)

Could you / Would you = more polite

Can you / Will you = less polite

NEVER "May you" - not possible!

Would you mind

Permission: + if clause (simple past)

Request: + gerund (-ing form)

Important distinction in form!

Examples:

  • Permission: May I have another portion?
  • Request: Could you please pass the salt?
  • Mind + permission: Would you mind if I ate some more?
  • Mind + request: Would you mind cooking this steak more?

Suggestions

Let's

= Let us

For a group including speaker

Negative: Let's not + base verb

Why don't

Can include speaker or not

Why don't we = includes speaker

Why don't you = excludes speaker

Shall

Very formal

Shall I or Shall we only

Asking for agreement on suggestion

Examples:

  • Let's go see a movie.
  • Let's not have chicken again.
  • Why don't we go see a movie?
  • Why don't you go see a movie? I'll stay home.
  • Shall I serve you some more?
  • Shall we go out tonight?

Final Review Summary

Necessity

must, have to, have got to

Negative: don't have to ≠ must not

Advisability

should, ought to, had better, could

Strength: had better > should/ought to > could

Expectation

be supposed to, should

What someone expects to happen

Ability

can, be able to, know how to

Physical or learned capability

Possibility

can, may, might

Degree of certainty varies

Requests & Suggestions

Politeness levels matter

Mind the form: gerund vs. if clause
