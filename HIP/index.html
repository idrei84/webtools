Executive Summary

This analysis examines the impact of newly implemented grammar labs on student performance in the Grammar Final Exam across three course levels: 460 (Intermediate), 1560 (High Intermediate), and 1585 (High Intermediate Writing & Grammar).

Key Finding: Overall, students in Spring 2025 (with labs) showed a +2.22 point improvement in mean exam scores compared to Fall 2024 (without labs).

Key Findings and Interpretations

1. Differential Impact by Course Level

  • Class 460 (Intermediate): Showed the strongest improvement with a statistically significant increase of 7.64 points (p = 0.025) and a medium effect size (d = 0.51). This suggests the labs were particularly effective for intermediate-level students.
  • Class 1560 (High Intermediate): Showed a slight decline of 3.14 points, though not statistically significant. The increased standard deviation (from 4.46 to 15.51) suggests more variable performance in Spring.
  • Class 1585 (Writing & Grammar): Showed minimal change with only a 0.84 point increase. The labs appear to have had little impact on this integrated skills course.

2. Possible Explanations

  • Skill Level Effect: The labs may be most beneficial for students at the intermediate level who are still solidifying fundamental grammar concepts.
  • Ceiling Effect: High intermediate students (1560) already had high baseline scores (89.71), leaving less room for improvement.
  • Course Focus: The 1585 course integrates writing with grammar, so pure grammar labs may have less direct impact.
  • Spring Term Factor: Despite the historical tendency for Spring performance to be slightly lower, the overall scores improved, suggesting the labs may have offset typical seasonal decline.

3. Statistical Considerations

  • While the overall effect wasn't statistically significant, the positive trend is encouraging, especially given the historical Spring decline.
  • The sample sizes are adequate for detecting medium to large effects but may be underpowered for small effects.
  • The reduction in score variability (lower SD) in Spring suggests more consistent performance, which could be attributed to the labs.

Recommendations

Based on these preliminary findings:

  1. Continue the labs, particularly for intermediate-level courses where the impact was most pronounced.
  2. Investigate the 1560 results to understand why high intermediate students didn't show improvement. Consider whether the labs need adjustment for this level.
  3. Collect additional data over more semesters to increase statistical power and account for semester-to-semester variation.
  4. Consider surveying students about their perception of the labs' helpfulness to gain qualitative insights.
  5. Analyze subgroups (e.g., by initial performance level) to identify which students benefit most from the labs.

Limitations

  • Sample sizes, while adequate, limit the ability to detect small effects.
  • Other factors between semesters (instructor variations, student populations) weren't controlled.
  • The analysis doesn't account for individual student characteristics or prior performance.
  • Missing data (zeros) were excluded, which could introduce bias if absences are related to the intervention.
