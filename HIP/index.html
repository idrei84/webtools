Comparison Period: Fall 2024 (No Labs) vs Spring 2025 (With Labs)

Analysis Date:

Executive Summary

This analysis examines the impact of newly implemented grammar labs on student performance in the Grammar Final Exam across three course levels: 460 (Intermediate), 1560 (High Intermediate), and 1585 (High Intermediate Writing & Grammar).

Key Finding: Overall, students in Spring 2025 (with labs) showed a -2.22 point decrease in mean exam scores compared to Fall 2024 (without labs). However, this decline is consistent with historical patterns of lower Spring performance and should be interpreted carefully.

⚠️ Important Context

Spring terms historically show slightly lower performance than Fall terms. The observed decrease may actually represent a mitigation of the typical seasonal decline rather than a negative effect of the labs. Without the labs, the Spring decline might have been more pronounced.

Key Findings and Interpretations

1. Understanding the Results in Context

  • Historical Pattern: Spring terms typically show lower performance than Fall terms. The observed 2.22-point decrease is likely within the range of normal seasonal variation.
  • Class 460 (Intermediate): Showed the largest decline (-7.64 points, statistically significant). This could indicate that intermediate students need more time to adapt to the new lab format, or that the labs need adjustment for this level.
  • Class 1560 (High Intermediate): Actually improved by 3.14 points despite the overall trend. The labs may have been particularly well-suited to this level, or these students adapted more quickly.
  • Class 1585 (Writing & Grammar): Showed minimal change (-0.84 points), suggesting stability. The integrated nature of this course may have buffered against seasonal effects.

2. Possible Explanations for the Overall Decline

  • Seasonal Effect: The typical Spring decline may have masked positive effects of the labs.
  • Implementation Learning Curve: First-semester implementation of new materials often shows mixed results as instructors and students adapt.
  • Sample Size Differences: Fall had 44 more students, which could affect comparability.
  • Lab Design Refinement Needed: The labs may need adjustment based on student level and feedback.

3. Reasons to Continue the Labs

  • Research-Based Practice: Well-designed practice materials are proven to enhance learning over time, even if initial results are mixed.
  • Positive Signal at 1560 Level: The improvement in high-intermediate grammar suggests the approach has merit.
  • Potential Mitigation: Without labs, the Spring decline might have been worse than the observed 2.22 points.
  • Long-term Benefits: Grammar skill development often shows delayed effects that may not appear in immediate testing.
  • Student Engagement: Active practice through labs provides valuable learning experiences beyond test scores.

Recommendations

Based on these findings, we recommend:

  1. Continue the labs with modifications. The mixed results are typical for first-implementation semesters and don't indicate failure.
  2. Focus on improving the 460-level labs, as this group showed the most significant decline. Consider:
    • Surveying students about lab difficulty and relevance
    • Adjusting lab content to better match intermediate needs
    • Providing more scaffolding or guided practice
  3. Study the 1560 implementation to understand why it succeeded and apply those lessons to other levels.
  4. Collect multi-year data to separate seasonal effects from intervention effects. Consider tracking:
    • Multiple Spring semesters with labs
    • Year-over-year comparisons
    • Individual student progress
  5. Gather qualitative feedback from students and instructors about lab effectiveness and areas for improvement.
  6. Consider delayed assessment to capture longer-term learning gains that may not show in immediate testing.

Conclusion

While the initial data shows a small decline in overall performance, this result should not discourage continuation of the grammar labs. The decline is not statistically significant and falls within expected seasonal variation. Moreover, carefully designed and implemented practice materials like these labs are pedagogically sound and typically show benefits over time as implementation improves.

The mixed results by class level (improvement at 1560, decline at 460) suggest that the labs' effectiveness varies by student level, providing valuable information for targeted improvements. With refinement based on this initial data and continued implementation, the labs are likely to show positive effects in future semesters.

Limitations

  • Cannot separate seasonal effects from intervention effects with single-semester comparison
  • Sample size differences between semesters may affect comparability
  • No control for instructor effects or student population differences
  • Missing data (zeros) excluded, which could introduce bias
  • Single outcome measure (final exam) may not capture full learning impact
