Grammar Lab Impact Analysis Report

Comparison Period: Fall 2024 (No Labs) vs Spring 2025 (With Labs)

Analysis Date: 05/23/2025

Executive Summary

This analysis examines the impact of newly implemented grammar labs on student performance in the Grammar Final Exam across three course levels: 460 (Intermediate), 1560 (High Intermediate), and 1585 (High Intermediate Writing & Grammar).

Key Finding: Overall, students in Spring 2025 (with labs) showed a modest decrease of 2.22 points in mean exam scores compared to Fall 2024 (without labs). This difference was not statistically significant (p = 0.199) and varies considerably by course level.

Key Findings and Interpretations

1. Differential Impact by Course Level

  • Class 460 (Intermediate): Showed a statistically significant decline of 7.64 points. This suggests that intermediate students may need more time to adapt to the new lab format, or that the labs require adjustment for this level.
  • Class 1560 (High Intermediate): Improved by 3.14 points, suggesting the labs were well-suited to this level. The dramatic reduction in standard deviation (15.51 to 4.46) indicates more consistent performance across students.
  • Class 1585 (Writing & Grammar): Showed minimal change (-0.84 points), indicating stability. The integrated nature of this course may respond differently to grammar-focused interventions.

2. Contextual Considerations

  • Implementation Learning Curve: First-semester implementation of new pedagogical materials often shows mixed results as instructors and students adapt to new approaches. Research in second language grammar instruction shows that explicit practice activities require time to show their full effect (Ellis, 2006; Norris & Ortega, 2000).
  • Sample Size Differences: The Fall semester had 44 more students than Spring, which could affect comparability and statistical power.
  • Semester Variations: Academic performance can vary between semesters due to multiple factors including student population differences, course scheduling, and external circumstances.

3. Reasons to Continue the Labs

  • Evidence-Based Practice: Form-focused instruction that includes structured practice activities has strong empirical support in second language acquisition research. Meta-analyses show that explicit grammar instruction combined with practice opportunities is more effective than implicit exposure alone, particularly for complex grammatical features (Norris & Ortega, 2000; Spada & Tomita, 2010).
  • Positive Signal at 1560 Level: The improvement in high-intermediate grammar demonstrates that the approach has merit and can be effective when well-matched to student level.
  • Formative Benefits: Regular practice through labs provides ongoing feedback opportunities that support learning beyond what final exam scores capture.
  • Skill Development: Grammar acquisition involves proceduralization of declarative knowledge through meaningful practice. The "grammaring" approach emphasizes that learners need multiple opportunities to use structures in context before achieving automaticity (Larsen-Freeman, 2003; Nassaji & Fotos, 2011).

Recommendations

Based on these findings, we recommend:

  1. Continue the labs with targeted modifications. The mixed results are typical for initial implementations and provide valuable data for improvement.
  2. Focus on refining the 460-level labs through:
    • Student feedback surveys about lab difficulty and relevance
    • Analysis of specific lab components that may need adjustment
    • Additional scaffolding or guided practice opportunities
    • Alignment review between lab content and exam assessments
  3. Study the successful 1560 implementation to identify best practices that can be adapted to other levels.
  4. Implement systematic data collection including:
    • Pre/post assessments within semesters
    • Tracking of lab completion and engagement
    • Multiple cohorts over several semesters
    • Qualitative feedback from students and instructors
  5. Consider lab design refinements based on contemporary research in grammar instruction and second language acquisition.
  6. Develop instructor resources to ensure consistent and effective lab implementation across sections.

Conclusion

While the initial aggregate data shows a small, non-significant decline in overall performance, the varied results by class level provide important insights for program development. The improvement at the 1560 level demonstrates that the labs can be effective, while the challenges at the 460 level highlight areas for refinement.

Well-designed practice materials grounded in active learning principles represent pedagogically sound approaches to grammar instruction. The current results, viewed as formative data from an initial implementation, provide a foundation for evidence-based improvements rather than a reason to discontinue the intervention. With targeted modifications and continued implementation, the labs have strong potential to enhance student learning outcomes across all levels.

Limitations

  • Single-semester comparison limits ability to distinguish intervention effects from other variables
  • Different sample sizes between semesters may affect statistical comparisons
  • No randomization or control group to isolate lab effects
  • Final exam scores may not capture all learning outcomes from lab participation
  • Student and instructor factors not controlled in the analysis
